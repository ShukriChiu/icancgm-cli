## 1. Longevity Diet Framework

Research basis: Harvard dietary patterns study (Nature Medicine, 2025) links
specific eating patterns to 20+ year lifespan extension. Blue Zones research
confirms plant-forward, minimally processed diets as key longevity factor.
Polyphenol-rich foods activate longevity genes (sirtuins, AMPK, mTOR pathways).

### The Longevity Diet Pattern

**Foundation (80–90% of plate):**
- Whole, minimally processed foods
- High fiber (40–50g daily)
- Omega-3 rich sources: fatty fish, flax, walnuts
- Polyphenol-rich foods: berries, green tea, dark chocolate, olive oil

**Moderate (daily):**
- Legumes and beans
- Nuts and seeds (1–2 oz)
- Whole grains (if tolerated)
- Fermented foods

**Minimal:**
- Animal protein (optional, <10% of calories)
- Refined carbohydrates and sugars
- Processed foods and seed oils
- Alcohol (if consumed, <1 drink/day)

**Avoid:**
- Ultra-processed foods
- Added sugars
- Trans fats
- Excessive sodium

### The Longevity Plate Template

Every meal should contain:

| Plate section | Portion | Foods |
|---------------|---------|-------|
| Non-starchy vegetables | 50% | Leafy greens, cruciferous (broccoli, cauliflower), colorful veg (peppers, tomatoes, carrots) |
| Protein | 25% | Fish (fatty preferred), poultry, legumes, eggs, tempeh/tofu |
| Smart carbs | 25% | Sweet potato, quinoa, brown rice (cooked & cooled), oats, legumes |
| Healthy fats (added) | drizzle/side | EVOO, ¼–½ avocado, 1–2 oz nuts/seeds |
| Flavor boosters | liberal | Turmeric + black pepper, garlic, ginger, herbs, lemon, vinegar |

## 2. Metabolic Health Optimization

Insulin sensitivity is the master lever of metabolic health. CGM reveals that
individual food responses vary dramatically. Meal timing and composition matter
as much as total calories. Metabolic flexibility (ability to switch between
burning fat and carbs) predicts longevity.

### Blood Sugar Regulation Strategies

- **Protein first** at meals (30–40g stabilizes glucose)
- **Fiber with every carb** source (slows absorption)
- **Vinegar before high-carb meals** (acetic acid improves insulin sensitivity)
- **Post-meal walk** (10 minutes reduces glucose spike by ~25%)
- **Meal sequencing**: Vegetables → Protein → Carbs

### Glycemic Targets

| Marker | Optimal | Watch | Address |
|--------|---------|-------|---------|
| Fasting glucose | 70–85 mg/dL | 86–99 | ≥100 |
| Post-meal peak | <120 mg/dL | 120–140 | >140 |
| Glucose variability | <30 mg/dL range | 30–45 | >45 |
| HbA1c | <5.4% | 5.4–5.6% | ≥5.7% |

### Insulin Sensitivity Enhancement

- Time-restricted eating (12–16 hour daily fast)
- Resistance training 3x/week (muscle is a glucose sink)
- High-fiber intake (40–50g daily feeds beneficial gut bacteria)
- Omega-3 supplementation (2–3g EPA/DHA daily)
- Magnesium adequacy (400–500mg daily)
- Quality sleep (7–9 hours; poor sleep = insulin resistance)

## 3. Gut Microbiome

The gut microbiome controls ~70% of immune function. Microbial metabolites
(SCFAs, secondary bile acids) regulate inflammation, mood, and metabolism.
Diversity of gut species predicts health outcomes better than any single marker.
Fermented foods outperform probiotics for microbiome diversity (Stanford, 2025).

### Prebiotics — Feed Beneficial Bacteria (daily)

| Type | Best sources |
|------|-------------|
| Resistant starch | Cooked & cooled potatoes/rice, green bananas |
| Inulin | Chicory root, Jerusalem artichoke, garlic, onions, asparagus |
| Pectin | Apples, citrus peels, berries |
| Beta-glucan | Oats, mushrooms, barley |
| Diverse fiber | 30+ different plant foods per week |

Target: 40–50g total fiber daily.

### Probiotics — Seed Beneficial Species (daily)

Fermented foods (whole foods > supplements for most people):
- Yogurt (live cultures, unsweetened)
- Kefir (highest probiotic diversity)
- Sauerkraut (raw, unpasteurized)
- Kimchi
- Kombucha
- Miso
- Tempeh

Probiotic supplements (when indicated):
- Multi-strain formulas (10+ billion CFU)
- Saccharomyces boulardii (antibiotic recovery)
- Lactobacillus + Bifidobacterium blends
- Soil-based / spore-forming strains

### Microbiome Disruptors to Avoid

- Artificial sweeteners (damage beneficial bacteria)
- Emulsifiers in processed foods (thin gut lining)
- Excessive antibiotics (destroy diversity)
- Chronic stress (cortisol alters microbiome)
- Ultra-processed foods (feed pathogenic bacteria)

## 4. Protein Optimization

Protein needs are higher than previously thought, especially for longevity.
The leucine threshold model: 2.5–3g leucine per meal triggers muscle protein
synthesis. Distribution across the day matters more than total.

### Daily Protein Targets

| Goal | Target (per lb bodyweight) | Example (150 lb person) |
|------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| Maintenance (sedentary) | 0.8–1.0g | 120–150g/day |
| Muscle building | 1.0–1.2g | 150–180g/day |
| Longevity (active aging) | 1.0–1.1g | 150–165g/day |
| Weight loss | 1.0–1.2g (goal weight) | 150–180g/day |

### Protein Distribution — Optimal Pattern

| Meal | Target | Rationale |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Breakfast | 30–40g | Hit leucine threshold early |
| Lunch | 30–40g | Sustain MPS |
| Dinner | 30–40g | Recovery window |
| Snack (if needed) | 20–30g | Bridge gaps |

### Leucine Content per Serving

| Food | Serving | Leucine | Hits threshold? |
|------|---------|---------|----------------|
| Whey protein | 25g | 3.0g | ✓ |
| Chicken breast | 6 oz | 3.0g | ✓ |
| Greek yogurt | 1 cup | 2.0g | close |
| Lentils | 1 cup | 1.3g | combine sources |

### Protein Quality

Complete proteins (all essential aminos): eggs, chicken, fish, beef, dairy,
soy, quinoa, buckwheat, hemp, chia.

Incomplete proteins (combine for completeness):
- Legumes + Grains (rice & beans = complete)
- Nuts/Seeds + Legumes

Both animal and plant proteins build muscle equally when the leucine threshold
is met per meal. Choose based on preference, ethics, and health goals.

## 5. Healthy Fats

Omega-3/Omega-6 ratio matters more than total fat. Source and context matter
for saturated fat. Trans fats remain toxic. Monounsaturated fats (olive oil,
avocado) are universally beneficial.

### Fat Quality Hierarchy

| Tier | Guidance | Foods |
|------|----------|-------|
| 1 — Liberal | Consume freely | EVOO (high polyphenol), avocados, fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), walnuts, almonds, macadamia, flax/chia/hemp seeds |
| 2 — Moderate | Reasonable amounts | Coconut oil (MCTs), grass-fed butter/ghee, dark chocolate (70%+), pasture-raised eggs |
| 3 — Minimize | Reduce intake | Conventional animal fats (grain-fed), seed oils heated at high temp |
| 4 — Eliminate | Zero tolerance | Trans fats, highly processed vegetable oils, margarine |

### Omega-3 Targets

Daily: 2–3g EPA/DHA (from fish or algae oil) + 1–2g ALA (from flax, chia, walnuts).

Best EPA/DHA sources per 6 oz serving:
- Wild salmon: 3–4g
- Sardines: 2–3g
- Mackerel: 3–4g
- Anchovies: 2g

Plant ALA converts to EPA/DHA at only ~5–10%:
- Ground flaxseed: 2.3g per tbsp
- Chia seeds: 2.5g per tbsp
- Walnuts: 2.5g per oz

Supplementation: Fish oil 2–3g EPA/DHA daily, or algae oil 1–2g (vegan).
Take with meals for absorption.

## 6. Carbohydrate Intelligence

Not all carbs are equal. Glycemic impact varies by individual (CGM reveals truth).
Carb timing matters for performance and body composition. Fiber is the most
important carb metric.

### Carb Quality Tiers

| Tier | Foods | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| 1 — Nutrient-dense | Sweet potatoes, quinoa, oats, berries, legumes, non-starchy vegetables | Daily staples |
| 2 — Whole food | Brown/wild rice, whole grain bread (sprouted), potatoes (cooked & cooled), whole fruit, winter squash | Good choices |
| 3 — Performance | White rice (post-workout), al dente pasta, sourdough, honey (pre-workout) | Context-dependent |
| 4 — Minimize | Added sugars, white bread, fruit juice, ultra-processed grains | Limit strictly |

### Carb Timing for Performance

| Window | Amount | Type | Example |
|--------|--------|------|---------|
| Pre-workout (1–2h before) | 30–60g | Low fiber, easy digestion | Banana + honey, white rice + fruit |
| Intra-workout (>90 min) | 30–60g/hour | Fast-absorbing | Glucose, sports drink |
| Post-workout (within 2h) | 0.5–1g/lb bodyweight | Glycogen replenishment, 3:1 carb:protein | Rice bowl + chicken |
| Rest days | Lower | Non-starchy veg focus | Save carbs for training days |

## 7. Hydration & Electrolytes

Even 2% dehydration impairs cognitive and physical performance. Electrolyte
balance matters more than pure water volume. Chronic low-grade dehydration
is epidemic.

### Daily Water Targets

Baseline: 0.5–1 oz per lb bodyweight (150 lb = 75–150 oz).

Adjustments:
- +12–16 oz per hour of exercise
- +20–40 oz in hot climate
- +1:1 replacement for caffeine/alcohol
- +20–30 oz on high protein diet

Timing:
- Morning: 16–32 oz upon waking
- Pre-workout: 16–20 oz (1–2h before)
- During workout: 7–10 oz every 10–20 min
- Post-workout: 20–24 oz per lb lost in sweat
- Throughout day: sip consistently

### Electrolyte Targets

| Electrolyte | Daily target | Best food sources |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Sodium | 3000–5000mg (higher if active) | Sea salt, miso, olives |
| Potassium | 3500–4700mg | Avocado, sweet potato, banana, coconut water |
| Magnesium | 400–500mg | Pumpkin seeds, dark chocolate, spinach, almonds |
| Calcium | 1000–1200mg | Dairy, sardines, leafy greens, fortified plant milk |

### DIY Electrolyte Drink

- 32 oz water
- ½ tsp sea salt (sodium)
- ¼ tsp potassium salt (NoSalt)
- Juice of 1 lemon
- Optional: 1–2 tsp honey (if training)

## 8. Supplements

Most supplements are unnecessary if diet is optimized. A few have robust
evidence. Quality and form matter (bioavailability). Personalize based on
blood work.

### Tier 1 — Nearly Universal Benefit

**Vitamin D3**
- Why: 40% of population deficient; immunity, mood, bone health
- Dose: 2000–5000 IU daily (test and adjust to 50–70 ng/mL)
- Form: D3 (cholecalciferol) with K2 for synergy
- Timing: with fat-containing meal

**Omega-3 (EPA/DHA)**
- Why: anti-inflammatory, brain, heart
- Dose: 2–3g EPA/DHA combined daily
- Form: triglyceride or phospholipid (better than ethyl ester)
- Quality: third-party tested for purity

**Magnesium**
- Why: 50% deficient; 300+ enzymatic reactions
- Dose: 400–500mg elemental
- Forms: glycinate (calming/sleep), threonate (brain), citrate (digestion)
- Timing: evening (promotes sleep)

**Creatine Monohydrate**
- Why: muscle strength, cognitive function, cell energy
- Dose: 5g daily (no loading needed)
- Timing: anytime

### Tier 2 — Targeted Benefit

| Supplement | When to use | Notes |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| Protein powder | Can't meet targets via food | Whey (post-workout), casein (bedtime), pea+rice (vegan) |
| Probiotics | Post-antibiotics, digestive issues | Multi-strain ≥10B CFU; whole fermented foods preferred |
| B-Complex | Vegan/vegetarian, high stress, 50+ | Methylated forms (methylcobalamin, methylfolate) |
| Vitamin K2 (MK-7) | Bone + cardiovascular health | 100–200 mcg daily, pair with D3 |

### Tier 3 — Performance Enhancement

| Supplement | Use case | Dose | Timing |
|-----------|----------|------|--------|
| Caffeine | Ergogenic aid | 3–6 mg/kg (200–400mg) | 30–60 min pre-workout |
| Beta-alanine | High-intensity endurance | 3–6g daily (split) | Causes harmless tingling |
| Citrulline malate | Blood flow, endurance | 6–8g | 30–60 min pre-workout |

## 9. Time-Restricted Eating & Fasting

Autophagy activates after 14–16 hours fasting. Metabolic switching (glucose →
ketones) improves metabolic health. Consistency matters more than perfection.
Not for everyone — women, high stress, and hard training require caution.

### Protocols

| Protocol | Fast/Eat | Example | Best for |
|----------|----------|---------|----------|
| 12:12 | 12h / 12h | 7am–7pm eat | Beginners, pregnancy, high training load |
| 14:10 | 14h / 10h | 8am–6pm eat | Women, moderate activity, sustainable long-term |
| 16:8 | 16h / 8h | 12pm–8pm eat | Most adults seeking metabolic health + fat loss |
| 24h fast | 24h / — | Dinner-to-dinner | Once per week/month, experienced fasters |
| 48–72h fast | Extended | Quarterly | Medical supervision, experienced only |

### Who Should NOT Fast

- Underweight individuals
- People with eating disorders
- Pregnant or breastfeeding
- Under high chronic stress
- Without medical guidance for extended fasts (>24h)

## 10. Anti-Inflammatory Nutrition

Chronic low-grade inflammation drives aging and disease. Diet is the primary
inflammation modulator. Omega-3/Omega-6 ratio is crucial (target 1:4 or better).
Polyphenols are anti-inflammatory superstars.

### Most Powerful Anti-Inflammatory Foods (daily)

- Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel)
- Berries (blueberries, strawberries, blackberries)
- Leafy greens (kale, spinach, arugula)
- Extra virgin olive oil (high-polyphenol)
- Turmeric (curcumin + black pepper for absorption)
- Ginger
- Green tea (EGCG)
- Dark chocolate (70%+ cacao)

Regular inclusion: nuts (walnuts, almonds), avocados, tomatoes (lycopene),
garlic & onions, mushrooms (beta-glucans), fermented foods, herbs & spices.

### Mechanisms

- Polyphenols → activate Nrf2 pathway → reduce oxidative stress
- Omega-3 → resolve inflammation via SPMs (specialized pro-resolving mediators)
- Fiber → feeds gut bacteria → produce anti-inflammatory SCFAs

### Most Inflammatory Foods (avoid/minimize)

Eliminate: trans fats, refined seed oils (heated), added sugars, ultra-processed foods.
Minimize: refined carbs, excessive alcohol (>1 drink/day), processed meats, grain-fed meat.

## 11. Practical Protocols

### Sample Day of Eating (Longevity-Optimized, ~2000 cal)

**Upon waking (7am):**
16–32 oz water with pinch of sea salt. Optional green tea.

**Breakfast (8am):**
3 eggs scrambled with turmeric + black pepper, sautéed spinach & mushrooms in
olive oil, ½ cup berries, ¼ avocado. → 30g protein, 15g carbs, 25g fat

**Lunch (12pm):**
Large salad (mixed greens, arugula), 6 oz grilled salmon, ½ cup quinoa,
½ cup chickpeas, olive oil + lemon dressing, 1 oz walnuts.
→ 45g protein, 40g carbs, 30g fat

**Snack (3pm, optional):**
1 cup unsweetened Greek yogurt, 1 tbsp chia seeds, ½ cup blueberries, 1 oz almonds.
→ 25g protein, 20g carbs, 15g fat

**Dinner (6pm):**
6 oz grass-fed beef or chicken thigh, large roasted vegetables (broccoli,
Brussels sprouts, carrots), 1 medium sweet potato, side salad with olive oil.
→ 40g protein, 45g carbs, 20g fat

**Evening (7–8pm):**
Herbal tea (chamomile, tulsi). Optional 1–2 squares dark chocolate (85%+).

**Daily totals:** ~140g protein (30%), ~120g carbs (30%), ~90g fat (40%)

**Supplements:**
- AM: Vitamin D3 5000 IU + K2 200 mcg, Omega-3 2g (with breakfast)
- PM: Magnesium glycinate 400mg (before bed)
- Post-workout: Creatine 5g

### Pre/Post Workout Nutrition

**Pre-workout (1–2h before):**
Goal: fuel without digestive distress.
- Option A: Banana + almond butter
- Option B: Oatmeal + berries + protein powder
- Option C: Greek yogurt + granola + fruit
- Hydration: 16–20 oz water, electrolytes if >60 min training

**During workout (>90 min):**
30–60g carbs/hour, electrolytes, sip 7–10 oz water every 10–20 min.

**Post-workout (within 2h):**
Goal: replenish glycogen, repair muscle. 30–40g protein + 0.5–1g carbs/lb bodyweight.
- Option A: Protein shake + banana + oats
- Option B: Chicken + rice + vegetables
- Option C: Salmon + sweet potato + greens
- Hydrate: 20–24 oz per lb lost in sweat

## 12. Special Populations

### Women's Nutrition

**Menstrual cycle phases:**

| Phase | Days | Nutrition shift |
|-------|------|----------------|
| Follicular (1–14) | Higher insulin sensitivity | Increase complex carbs, support intense training |
| Luteal (15–28) | Lower carb tolerance | Increase protein/fats, +100–300 cal, magnesium for PMS |

**Pregnancy/postpartum:** +300–500 cal, emphasize folate, iron, choline, DHA.
Avoid fasting. Prioritize nutrient density.

**Menopause:** Increase protein (preserve muscle), calcium + vitamin D (bone),
phytoestrogens (flax, soy) may help. Resistance training is crucial.

### Athletes & Hard Trainers

- Protein: 1.0–1.2g/lb
- Carbs: 2–4g/lb (sport-dependent)
- Hydration: +12–16 oz/hour training
- Electrolytes: sodium 3000–5000mg, potassium 4000mg+
- Nutrient timing is critical (pre/intra/post)
- Consider: creatine, beta-alanine, caffeine, tart cherry

### Aging Adults (50+)

- Higher protein: 1.0–1.2g/lb (preserve muscle)
- Calcium + Vitamin D (bone health)
- Omega-3 (cognitive function, inflammation)
- B12 (absorption decreases with age)
- Leucine-rich foods (counter sarcopenia)
- Resistance training (non-negotiable)
- May need digestive enzymes

## 13. Data-Driven Personalization

This section explains how to translate health data into specific nutritional
adjustments. For data source connections, CLI commands, and raw interpretation
targets, see `data-sources.md`.

### The Personalization Loop

Generic advice is a starting point. Real value comes from iterating with data:

1. **Baseline** — Establish current eating pattern and collect initial data
   (CGM, blood work, wearable metrics)
2. **Identify** — Find the top 1–2 issues the data reveals (glucose spikes?
   poor sleep? inflammation markers high?)
3. **Intervene** — Apply one targeted change from this guide's protocols
4. **Measure** — Recheck the same data after 2–4 weeks
5. **Adjust** — If improved, maintain and move to next priority. If not,
   try a different intervention.

The key: change one variable at a time so you know what's working.

### CGM-Guided Meal Optimization

CGM data is the fastest feedback loop for nutrition. A 2-week CGM experiment
reveals more about someone's individual food responses than months of generic
dieting.

**What to look for and what to change:**

| CGM pattern | Likely cause | Nutrition intervention |
|-------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Post-meal spike >140 mg/dL | Carbs without buffer | Meal sequencing (veg → protein → carbs), add fiber + fat |
| Slow glucose return (>3 hours) | Insulin resistance | Reduce portion size, add post-meal walk, consider TRE |
| Morning spike (dawn phenomenon) | Cortisol-driven glucose dump | Evening protein snack, ACV before bed, earlier dinner |
| Overnight instability | Late eating, alcohol, stress | Stop eating 3h before bed, no alcohol, magnesium before sleep |
| Flat-high baseline (>100 fasting) | Chronic insulin resistance | Full metabolic protocol: TRE + fiber + resistance training + sleep |
| Post-workout spike | Normal stress response | Usually fine — confirm it returns to baseline within 1–2h |
| Different response to same food | Gut microbiome, sleep, stress | Track sleep and stress alongside meals to find the real driver |

**The meal experiment protocol:**
1. Eat a test meal while wearing CGM
2. Note glucose at 30, 60, 90, 120 minutes post-meal
3. Try the same base meal with modifications:
   - Add vegetables before → compare spike
   - Add protein before → compare spike
   - Walk 10 min after → compare spike
   - Swap carb source → compare spike
4. Build a personal "safe meals" list of foods that keep glucose stable

### Sleep-Nutrition Connection

Poor sleep (< 7 hours or low quality) is one of the strongest drivers of
metabolic dysfunction. When wearable data shows poor sleep:

| Sleep issue | Metabolic impact | Nutrition response |
|------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Short duration (<7h) | +25% insulin resistance next day | Lower carbs, higher protein/fat on sleep-deprived days |
| Low deep sleep % | Poor recovery, elevated cortisol | Magnesium glycinate 400mg before bed, no caffeine after 2pm |
| Low REM % | Cognitive impairment | Cut evening caffeine window further, consider glycine 3g |
| Late sleep onset | Circadian misalignment | Stop eating 3h before target bedtime |
| Fragmented sleep | Gut + inflammation | Evening chamomile tea, eliminate alcohol, check for food sensitivities |

### Activity-Matched Nutrition

When activity data is available, match fuel to output:

| Activity level | Carb strategy | Protein priority |
|---------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Rest day / low activity | Minimal carbs, focus on non-starchy veg | Standard (0.8–1g/lb) |
| Moderate training (30–60 min) | Include smart carbs at meals near workout | Higher (1g/lb) |
| Intense training (>60 min) | Pre + post workout carbs essential | Highest (1–1.2g/lb) |
| High step count but no training | Normal carb level | Standard |
| Two-a-days / competition | Aggressive carb loading | Highest + recovery focus |

### Blood Work → Supplement Decision Tree

Use actual lab values rather than defaulting to the standard supplement stack.

```
Vitamin D < 30 ng/mL → 5000 IU D3 + K2 daily, retest in 3 months
Vitamin D 30–50 ng/mL → 2000 IU D3 + K2 daily
Vitamin D 50–70 ng/mL → maintenance dose or skip

Omega-3 Index < 4% → 3g EPA/DHA daily + fatty fish 3x/week
Omega-3 Index 4–8% → 2g EPA/DHA daily
Omega-3 Index > 8% → maintain with diet

hs-CRP > 3.0 → Full anti-inflammatory protocol
hs-CRP 1–3 → Increase polyphenols and omega-3
hs-CRP < 1.0 → Maintain current approach

Fasting insulin > 10 → Aggressive metabolic intervention (TRE + fiber + training)
Fasting insulin 5–10 → Moderate intervention (meal sequencing + fiber)
Fasting insulin < 5 → Optimal, maintain

Ferritin < 30 (women) or < 50 → Iron-rich foods + vitamin C for absorption
Ferritin 50–150 → Optimal
Ferritin > 300 → Reduce red meat, consider blood donation, check inflammation
```

### Combining Multiple Data Sources

The most powerful insights come from overlaying data:

**CGM + Sleep data:**
Compare glucose patterns on good-sleep vs poor-sleep nights. Most people see
20–40% worse glucose control after poor sleep. This makes the case for sleep
as a metabolic intervention — sometimes fixing sleep does more than changing diet.

**CGM + Activity data:**
Track which types of exercise best improve next-day fasting glucose. Resistance
training often has a stronger 24–48h glucose-lowering effect than cardio.

**CGM + Meal logging:**
Build a personal glycemic index. The standard GI tables are population averages —
individual responses vary by 2–3x. Your CGM tells you YOUR response.

**Blood work + CGM + Wearable:**
The complete picture. Blood work shows the long-term trajectory (HbA1c, inflammation),
CGM shows the daily patterns, and wearables show the lifestyle factors (sleep, stress,
activity) driving those patterns. Start with the lifestyle factors — they're the
root cause more often than diet alone.

## 14. Common Mistakes

**Fearing all carbs.** Excess calories cause fat gain, not carbs per se. Choose
nutrient-dense carbs, time them around training, prioritize fiber.

**Neglecting protein distribution.** Eating 100g at dinner and 10g at breakfast
means suboptimal muscle protein synthesis. Distribute 30–40g per meal.

**Chronic low-calorie dieting.** Perpetually eating <1500 cal causes metabolic
adaptation, hormonal disruption, muscle loss. Reverse diet slowly, incorporate
diet breaks (2 weeks at maintenance every 12 weeks).

**Overcomplicating nutrition.** Obsessing over timing, macros, and supplements
leads to paralysis. Master fundamentals first: whole foods, protein, vegetables,
hydration. 80/20 rule. Consistency > perfection.

**Ignoring micronutrients.** Hitting macros but eating the same foods daily
leads to deficiencies. Eat the rainbow. 30+ different plant foods per week.

## 15. Core Principles

1. **Nutrient density over calorie counting** — Quality first, quantity second.
2. **Personalization is key** — Discover your optimal diet through CGM, blood work, and self-experimentation.
3. **Gut health is foundation** — Diverse fiber, fermented foods, minimal processed food. 70% of immune function lives here.
4. **Protein is non-negotiable** — Especially as you age. Muscle is longevity.
5. **Consistency > perfection** — The "good enough" diet you stick to beats the "perfect" one you don't.
6. **Food is information** — Every meal sends signals to genes, hormones, and cells.
7. **Eat for longevity, not just performance** — Play the infinite game.
8. **Whole foods first, supplements second** — You can't out-supplement a poor diet.
9. **Mindful eating matters** — Chew thoroughly, eat slowly, minimize stress while eating.
10. **Flexibility prevents obsession** — 80/20 rule. Orthorexia is its own disorder.
